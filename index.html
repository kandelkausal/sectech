Understanding patterns in data and the world around you

    Patterns everywhere

    A pattern is something that follows a rule you can describe and reuse. Once you know the rule, you can explain what you have seen and make a reasonable guess about what might come next.

    Patterns appear in nature — seasons, tides, the phases of the moon. They appear in music when a chorus returns. They appear in code when a program repeats the same steps for every item in a list.

    Random vs patterned

    7 2 9 1 hard to predict
    10 20 30 40 add 10 each time

    In Digital Technologies, pattern recognition is part of computational thinking: organising information logically, spotting what stays the same, and describing the steps that produce a result.

    Computers are fast at applying a rule millions of times. People are good at finding the rule in the first place.

    Sequences and rules

    A sequence is an ordered list where position matters. Each item is called a term. The rule tells you how one term relates to the ones around it.

    Think of footprints in sand: each step comes after the last. If the stride is regular, you can picture the next footprint before you reach it. That is the same kind of thinking used when reading sensor data, animation frames, or rows in a spreadsheet.

    A sequence with a clear rule

    MonTueWedThuFri?

    The rule comes from a familiar cycle — days of the week.

    Some rules connect neighbouring terms (what changes from one to the next?). Other rules connect each term to earlier terms (what history does this term depend on?). Naming which kind you are dealing with is often the first breakthrough.

    Steady change

    Many number patterns grow or shrink by the same amount every step. The gap between neighbours is constant.

    Growing by 5

    49141924

    Each term is 5 more than the one before.

    Shrinking by 3

    4037343128

    Each term is 3 less than the one before.

    Temperature dropping through an evening, a countdown timer, or a stack of blocks removed one at a time — all can follow steady change. When you see this kind of pattern, write down the gap. If it is always the same, you have found a large part of the rule.

    Cycles that repeat

    A repeating pattern uses the same block of elements over and over, in the same order. The block is called the unit or cycle.

    Stripes in design

    Two colours alternating — a cycle of length two.

    Pixel art, weaving, and loading animations on a screen often rely on short cycles. To analyse a repeating pattern, ask: How long is the unit before it starts again?

    Each step builds on the last

    Not every pattern adds the same amount each time. Some patterns multiply — each term is double the previous one. Others combine earlier terms to create the next.

    Doubling

    36122448

    Each term × 2. Common in growth models and computer memory sizes.

    Combining the two before

    112358

    Each new term = sum of the two terms before it. Found in nature, art, and architecture.

    When neighbours do not share a simple gap, look further back. Does the new value depend on one previous term or on several?

    Two stories in one line

    Sometimes a single row hides two patterns at once, woven together. One pattern lives in the odd positions; another lives in the even positions.

    Split the row into two tracks

    Track A (1st, 3rd, 5th…) ACEG
    Track B (2nd, 4th, 6th…) 10203040
    A10C20E30G40

    The combined row looks mixed — until you separate the threads.

    Audio and video interleaving, or merging two data streams into one file, use similar ideas. When a sequence feels “messy,” try separating odd and even positions before you decide there is no rule.

    Rules that take turns

    Some patterns use two different rules that alternate: first rule A, then rule B, then rule A again. The change from one term to the next is not always the same — but the pair of changes may repeat.

    Size and shape alternate

    Compare first-to-second, second-to-third, third-to-fourth. If you see two different operations cycling, you are likely looking at an alternating rule set.

    Investigating a pattern

    When the rule is hidden, use a calm, repeatable process — the same kind of careful thinking programmers use before writing code.

    1. Look at the whole sequence. Do not focus on a single gap too early. Context from earlier and later terms matters.
    2. Describe what changes. Note gaps, shapes, or relationships between neighbours and between terms further apart.
    3. Propose a rule in words. “Adds 5 each time,” “repeats red-blue,” “odd spots count by 2…”
    4. Test on terms you already know. If your rule fails on an existing term, revise it — do not force it to fit.
    5. Explain your reasoning. Being able to justify a pattern is as important as spotting it. That explanation can become an algorithm someone else — or a computer — can follow.

    Pattern recognition is not about quick guessing. It is about building a clear story for why the sequence behaves the way it does — in mathematics, in science, and in every digital system you will design or use.
